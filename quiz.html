Quiz

/Redesign/

Redesigning a guided buying quiz

The Client

Larson Doors is a leading storm door retailer in the U.S. They sell directly to consumers through their website, as well as through independent dealers and major retailers like Lowe’s, Menards, and Amazon. As part of a broader rebranding initiative, Larson aimed to redesign both their digital and in-store presence.

The Challenge

As part of the website redesign team, I analyzed Google Analytics reports to assess the site's performance, identifying top pages, user engagement, and overall usability. I discovered that the guided buying quiz, introduced just a year earlier, had low engagement.


To determine its value, I met with the Larson Doors team, who saw it as a key conversion tool and strongly supported its inclusion in the new website. The goal was to redesign the quiz experience to align with the new branding, increase usage and click-through rates, and drive conversions, all while ensuring responsiveness across desktop, tablet, and mobile.

Research and Planning

With a detailed brief in hand, I needed to understand why users weren’t engaging with the quiz. I started by conducting user testing on our target audience to observe their shopping behaviors on the website. Once they reached the quiz, I focused on their ease of navigation, potential pain points, and actions taken after completing it.


I reviewed the company’s personas and identified 23 participants who matched our audience. Testing was conducted across mobile, tablet, and desktop. I set up two tasks:


  • 1. Asking users to purchase a storm door on our website.

  • 2. Directing them to the quiz and observing their experience.

During both tasks, I encouraged participants to think out loud as they navigated the site, allowing me to capture insights in real time. After collecting the data, I synthesized the findings to identify patterns and recurring issues that impacted the user experience. This analysis provided a clear direction for refining the quiz.

Key Findings

  • Users weren’t noticing the quiz callout on the homepage, explaining the low engagement rate.

  • The preferred way to shop was by style.

  • The quiz’s language was unclear, leading to confusion and hesitation.

  • Users lacked contextual information, making decisions difficult.

While I researched best practices for quiz usability—including insights from Nielsen Norman Group and UX Collective—the most valuable guidance came directly from the user testing results. The patterns and pain points uncovered in testing were the driving force behind the design decisions, ensuring that changes were rooted in real user behavior rather than just industry recommendations.

Designing the Solution

User Flows

To address the issue of users not finding the quiz, I collaborated with the design team to test three potential placements: the homepage, support center, and project center. The team also worked on a new callout design and placement strategy to improve visibility.

User testing revealed that the quiz content was confusing and overwhelming. To simplify the experience, I introduced an additional page to separate style options from features and functionalities.

The original quiz allowed users to choose their starting point, but testing showed hesitation in making an initial selection. To reduce cognitive load, I restructured the flow with predetermined steps, starting with style options.

Early Sketching

With strong research insights, I began ideating on the layout and interaction design. While option logic was predetermined by the data team, I had flexibility to refine copy, styling, and layout. For inspiration, I analyzed guided buying quizzes from brands like Prose, Casper, and Clare (hyperlinks). Interestingly, I found no strong door- buying quiz examples, reinforcing the need for a well-designed experience.

User testing also provided direct insights, such as users struggling to select colors on mobile due to small swatch sizes. Using these findings, I sketched multiple concepts for layout and interaction.

Writing detailed design and component briefs, often accompanied by wireframes. I worked closely with the designers through the development process, providing constructive feedback to ensure that the user experience and behaviors were at the forefront of every design decision.

Wireframes

Once I had explored different approaches, I created paper wireframes to present to my UX team for first-round feedback. The focus was on validating the user flow and ease of navigation.

High-Fidelity Prototypes

After iterating on feedback from multiple review rounds, I developed high-fidelity prototypes to present to stakeholders, offering variations for approval before moving into development.

Conclusion

The redesigned guided buying quiz was well received by stakeholders and successfully handed off to the development team for implementation. With thoughtful attention to every design detail, I was able to clearly articulate the rationale behind my decisions, ensuring alignment with both user needs and business goals.

What I’d Do Differently

Given more time, I would have liked to:


  • Create an interactive prototype for early-stage usability testing.

  • Conduct additional user testing post-implementation to measure improvements in engagement and conversion rates.

Key Takeaways

This project involves numerous teams, including Data, Commercialization, Direct to Consumer, Channel Marketing, and many others. I was able to gain a wicked amount of first-hand UX/UI experience.


  • Visibility matters – even the most well-designed features won’t succeed if users can’t find them.

  • Simplification is powerful – reducing decision-making complexity improved the user experience.

  • User feedback is invaluable – real user insights drove design decisions far more than best practices alone.

By integrating research, user testing, and iterative design, I created a more intuitive, user-friendly quiz that aligned with the new branding and supported business objectives.
